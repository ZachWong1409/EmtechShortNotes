Chapter 7
Technology Assessment & Strategy Making
Step 1
Scoping
Strategic Intent · Firm Capabilities
What scoping does
Establishes the boundaries of the assessment before any technology is evaluated. Technology cannot be considered in the abstract — it must answer a question or address a challenge. Where the firm looks for technology depends entirely on what it's looking for. "If you don't have a target, you'll miss it every time."
Two halves of scoping
Strategic intent
Assessed by the firm's strategic intent. Defines the target market and the potential threat or opportunity the technology represents. Tech assessment is only meaningful in the context of this intent.
Firm capabilities
Assessed by the firm's core capabilities. Codifying technical capabilities is the starting point for the whole assessment.
Examples of strategic intent ← direct review Q
British Airways
Goal of becoming "The World's Favorite Airline."
New business unit
Meet this year's goal of launching a new $200 million business unit.
Product shelf life
Increase product shelf life.
Production costs
Lower production costs.
What new information systems could help British Airways become the world's favorite airline? — the strategic intent turned into a technology question.
Core competence — what it could be
Special technical capabilities leading to unique products.
Ability to provide superior services.
Capabilities for rapid product innovation and development.
Particular capabilities yielding enduring cost advantages.
First-rate technology assessment can itself be a crucial core competence in today's economy.
Key questions for firm capabilities
1
Does the firm possess sufficient capabilities?
2
Will it have to acquire, develop, or create partnerships to attain specific capabilities for tech development and commercialization? (may also require divesting existing capabilities.)
Managing a creative technology process requires balancing the firm's capabilities & constraints with its intellectual openness to new technologies and ambitions for growth.

Step 2
Searching
Sensing Emergence · Strong & Weak Signals · Knowledge Capture
What searching does
Determine the information and technology sources that need to be consistently reviewed for signs of technology change. Set up procedures and organizational arrangements to screen technologies. Search for signals of both emergent technology and its commercial viability.
Sensing technological emergence ← exam favorite
The challenge is recognizing momentum forming around a given technology. Leading technologies are recognized as emergent by their technological "following." Technology forecasting works by looking into the recent past for signals of momentum building behind a technology toward filling a market need.
Strong vs weak signals ← direct review Q
Strong signals
Clearly reveal commercial investment in the candidate technology and signal its technical feasibility to serve target market needs.

Examples: patent & literature citations · competitor's actions.
Weak signals
More subtle indicators that a discovery has commercial potential, that independent analysis has recognized this, and that a following has formed.

Examples: confirmation within knowledge networks · competitive intelligence.
Knowledge & information capture — why it matters
Information gathered at the start may only be fully understood later on.
Technologies continue to progress and develop over time.
As the team grows or changes, compiled info brings new members up to speed fast.
Internet & corporate intranets create extraordinary capture opportunities.
The team's rationale for decisions should be explicitly described — this monitors progress and earns leadership support for new initiatives.
Key terms to know
Technology forecastingTechnological followingStrong signalsWeak signalsPatent citationsCompetitive intelligenceKnowledge capture
Step 3
Evaluating
Ranking Criteria · Risk Profiling · Technology Plans
What evaluating does
Sorts through the set of possibilities defined during searching. Managers rank candidate technologies on a common set of financial & organizational criteria. Technology positioning is achieved by weighting scores across these criteria — drafting the financial, competitive, and organizational impacts of the technologies analyzed.
Ranking criteria
Financial analysis
Projected returns, investment required, financial impact of the candidate technology.
Measures of risk
Risk profiling — mapping risk against reward for each candidate technology.
Technology plans — what they map out
1
Technology commercialization — the path to bringing it to market.
2
Required investment — how much and when.
3
Organizational implications — what it means for structure and people.
4
Potential financial rewards — the expected payoff.
A draft plan reveals outstanding questions that must be answered to reduce uncertainty. It describes technical & market uncertainties and the timing of investments toward resolving each.
Don't forget the rejects
It's important to limit technology choices, but also to preserve a record of technologies that didn't make the grade — they may resurface as a future possible breakthrough.

Financial analysisRisk profilingWeighting scoresTechnology planCommercialization pathOrganizational implications

Strategy Making
Planning Under Uncertainty & Disciplined Imagination
Art of Strategy · Discipline · Imagination · The Combination
Planning under uncertainty
Needs to be rethought in an environment of rapid change. High uncertainty is characteristic, and markets demand new solutions to new problems. Mechanical planning no longer works — firms must adjust strategies quickly and create new ones continually.
The art of strategy making
Strategy is an art, not a science — "a theory of success that has not yet been tested." The strategist must choose one among many competing strategies for the actual test; not all possible strategies can be validated by implementing each one. No amount of planning guarantees success, but it improves the odds. Past successful approaches remain an option for the future.
"A set of assumptions about markets, technology, and its dynamics, about a company's strengths and weaknesses, and about what the company gets paid for." — Peter Drucker, on the "theory of business"
Twin pillars of strategy ← direct review Q
Discipline
Consistent application of rules to evaluate a set of alternatives. Achieved by identifying patterns in market evolution and comparing them to similar markets.
Imagination
Associated with synthesis, vision, foresight, creativity, intuition. A deliberate effort to generate and evaluate more options — "deliberate diversity" in how problems are defined and solutions generated.
Discipline — benefits & limitations
Benefits
Linked to greater consensus among top managers · helps managers be more judicious · identifies and corrects "avoidable errors" · more exhaustive/inclusive decision process.
Limitations
Analysis rather than synthesis · selection at the expense of generation · extrapolation from the past · overconfidence in the power of analysis.
Imagination — the process
Alternatives must be varied and distinct, not mere variations on a theme.
Use multiple cognitive frames to examine and define the problem.
Diversity in the rules used to select among alternatives.
"Future-oriented" — imagine the destination and work backward.
Maintain a portfolio of options as "launching pads" to adapt quickly.
Imagination — benefits & limitations
Benefits
Adaptive thinking, persistent "thinking out-of-the-box" · abandons the "hierarchy of experience" for a "hierarchy of imagination."
Limitations
Chaos · losing touch with reality · undervaluing the past · diluting individual creativity · slowing the process.
Disciplined imagination — the combination
Combination rather than extreme emphasis on either pillar. Capitalizes on the strength of each while avoiding their weaknesses. Imagination generates diverse options and encourages creative thinking; discipline grounds the process in reality so options get evaluated, developed, and implemented.
"Movies are a make believe business that feeds on imagination. When you are creating them, you are in the fantasy world. Without some kind of discipline, however, it is too easy to get distracted." — George Lucas
Practicing disciplined imagination
1
Generate imaginative options.
2
Evaluate the options consistently.
3
Alternate periods of imaginative idea generation with periods of disciplined idea evaluation.
Sometimes requires a more complex route: dismount the formal present system, build a new process from scratch — new space, new rules, new lenses, challenge the orthodoxies.
Conclusions
A rigorous, iterative process for assessing technologies is crucial — mistakes carry expensive strategic and financial consequences.
High uncertainty is a defining characteristic of technology assessment.
Disciplined imagination is common across artists, scientists, theorists, and strategists venturing into the unknown.
Firms should develop both capabilities, but need not deploy them simultaneously.
Key terms to know
Theory of businessDisciplineImaginationDisciplined imaginationHierarchy of imaginationStrategic posture
Four-step process = Scoping → Searching → Evaluating → Committing. Strong signals (patents, competitor actions) vs weak signals (knowledge network confirmation, competitive intel). Twin pillars = Discipline + Imagination, combined as "disciplined imagination."

Ethics & Professionalism of Emerging Technologies
Section 1
Technology & Ethics
The Internet's Double Edge · New Ethical Questions · Ethics Arriving Too Late
Technology's double edge
The internet boom enabled new tools and new ways for people to interact, but it wasn't without downsides: it raised new privacy concerns, got hampered by spam and viruses, and while it connects people globally, it also threatens to cut off those who lack access to it (digital divide).
Technology can promote or restrict human rights
The information society should foster emerging technology use to maximize benefits while minimizing harms. This requires a proper legal/regulatory system to ensure technology capable of abuse isn't actually abused, and that the benefits of technology are shared among all, not concentrated.
5 basic principles of professional ethics codes
Integrity
Objectivity
Competence and due care
Confidentiality
Professional behavior
Emerging technologies raise new challenges against these five — they didn't disappear, but applying them gets harder with AI/big data involved.
New ethical questions ← direct review Q
Big data, algorithmic decision-making, and AI enable consistent, evidence-based, fast and efficient decisions. But this strength has a darker side:
Biased outputs
Outputs can be based on biased data, leading to discriminatory outcomes.
Recreating the past
Systems that learn from real-world data risk simply recreating the past, building in old errors or systemic biases.
Human-machine interaction questions
1
How much reliance can be placed on data and models?
2
What is the role of human judgement?
3
How do we ensure we understand the decision-making process (not a black box)?
Whatever the power of machines, humans must still be involved — so people can be held accountable, or explain the reasons behind a decision.
Ethics arrives too late ← key conceptual point
A central problem: ethics of technology tends to arrive too late. Ethical issues are often only recognized once the technology is already on the market and problems arise during widespread usage. Ethics has traditionally been poorly equipped to deal with uncertainty, especially future uncertainty — exactly the condition emerging tech operates in.

Section 3
Digital Privacy
Information · Communication · Individual Privacy + Principles
What digital privacy is
Digital Privacy refers to the protection of personally identifiable or business identifiable information collected through information collection activities or other sources. It's a collective term covering three sub-categories, and is often used in contexts advocating for individual and consumer privacy rights in digital spheres.
3 sub-categories ← direct review Q
Information privacy
The right to determine how your personally identifiable digital information is collected and used by companies. Laws vary by country — e.g. Malaysia's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA).
Communication privacy
The right to communicate digitally with the expectation of security. Communications can be intercepted or misdelivered without the sender knowing — often due to not reading the platform's privacy policy.
Individual privacy
The right to exist freely online and choose what information you're exposed to. Breaches: unwanted ads/spam, viruses forcing unwanted actions — both interrupt a person's ability to exist digitally undisturbed.
Digital privacy principles (from lecture slide) ← direct review Q
PrincipleExplanation
Data minimizationCollect the minimal amount of information necessary from individuals/businesses, consistent with the organization's mission and legal requirements.
TransparencyProvide clear notice of the purpose of collection and use of identifiable information. Information won't be used for any other purpose unless authorized or mandated by law.
AccuracyInformation collected will be maintained sufficiently accurate, timely, and complete to protect the interests of individuals and businesses.
SecurityAdequate physical and IT security measures implemented so collection, use, and maintenance of identifiable info are safeguarded, and info is destroyed promptly per approved records schedules.

Section 4
Accountability & Trust
The Paradox of Risk-Management Tools
Why accountability & trust matter
Emerging tech creates far-reaching, rapid change, and brings new forms of risk. Understanding and mitigating these risks builds confidence in ET. ET can give businesses accuracy, better quality, and cost efficiencies in every sector, and can enhance trust in an organization's operations and financial processes — crucial for sustainable success.
The paradox ← key conceptual point, very examinable
The very solutions used to better manage risk, increase transparency, and build confidence are often themselves the source of new risk — risk that may go unnoticed. There's also a danger that relying on technology degrades people's willingness to judge and personally intervene, since they're not personally connected to the consumers and outcomes affected.
Definition of accountability
The obligation of an organization or individual to account for its activities, accept responsibility for them, and disclose the results in a transparent manner.

Threats & Challenges
By Technology · By Risk Type
Security as a constant challenge
With technology moving fast, security has always been a challenge. Growing cyber and data security needs require security practitioners to focus on business needs for securing data, understanding security risk from a business perspective, and extensively interacting with the business community to understand what they actually need.
Challenges in using ET, by technology ← direct review Q
TechnologyChallenge
Artificial IntelligencePotential to replace human workers in higher-level jobs as machines get more "intelligent." Alternatively, AI can take over laborious/time-consuming tasks, freeing time for other work — e.g. doctors using diagnostic AI still analyze the data and make the final decision.
RoboticsSame job-loss risk as AI, intensified as automation moves from production lines into other business areas. As robots learn to teach themselves and each other, productivity rises, but so do ethical and cybersecurity concerns.

Chapter 12
Avoiding Pitfalls of Emerging Technologies
Section 1
Common Traps Facing Established Firms
4 Traps that Disadvantage Incumbents
The setup
Established firms (incumbents) face common traps that put them at a disadvantage when emerging technologies (ETs) appear. These traps explain why big, successful companies often get beaten to the punch by newer rivals when a new technology shows up.
4 traps that disadvantage incumbents ← direct review Q, very examinable
1. Delayed participation
Waiting too long to get involved with the ET, often justified as "rational" caution under uncertainty.
2. Sticking with the familiar
Choosing a known but possibly wrong technology path instead of the unfamiliar new one.
3. Reluctance to fully commit
Investing half-heartedly because full commitment threatens existing profitable products.
4. Lack of persistence
Giving up early when short-term financial results disappoint, even though new tech often needs a long gestation period.
Trap 1: Delayed participation
High uncertainty makes "watch and wait" feel rational. Managers operate from mental models that impose order on unclear situations to make them more manageable. Delaying participation is only suitable for narrow applications not yet required by existing customers — early, immature iterations of most ETs are easy to dismiss (e.g. early electronic watches). The trap: this approach is not suitable once the technology starts to matter to existing customers.
Trap 2: Sticking with the familiar
The odds of picking a familiar but wrong technology path go up when:
1
Past success reinforces established ways of problem-solving and decision-making, leading the firm to search only in areas closely related to its current skills and technologies.
2
The firm lacks in-house capability to fully appraise the new ET.
3
A proprietary mind-set gets in the way.
Sticking with the known becomes even more appealing — and riskier — when multiple technologies are advancing simultaneously. The most worrying choices are those where multiple competing versions are vying to become the dominant design, with firms competing to set the industry standard for lasting advantage.
Trap 3: Reluctance to fully commit
New ETs can cannibalize existing profitable products, which brings resistance from channel partners. This produces bold forecasts on one hand but timid choices on the other — investments are hard to justify under strict ROI criteria. Firms stay focused on meeting current customers' needs rather than serving new needs and new markets. The underlying issue: successful organisations are not naturally "ambidextrous" (skilled at both exploiting the old and exploring the new at once).
Trap 4: Lack of persistence
Facing pressure for quarterly results, firms soon lose patience with adverse outcomes. Missed forecasts and dashed hopes are commonly experienced during the gestation of new technologies that eventually succeed. Example: Knight Ridder, Inc. (a major U.S. newspaper company) repeatedly withdrew from early moves into ETs such as electronic publishing, online news, and interactive services in response to negative financial results.
If a scenario describes a firm pulling out of a promising ET after a few bad quarters, that's Trap 4 (lack of persistence) — not Trap 1, even though both involve hesitation. Persistence is about quitting after starting; delayed participation is about not starting at all.

Section 2
Four Solutions to Avoid the Traps
Peripheral Vision · Learning Culture · Flexibility · Autonomy
4 possible solutions ← direct review Q, very examinable
1. Widening peripheral (indirect) vision
Looking beyond the obvious, direct signals to spot ETs that matter strategically.
2. Creating a learning culture
Building organisational (not just individual) capacity to keep learning from weak signals.
3. Staying flexible in strategic ways
Keeping commitments reversible so direction can change quickly when needed.
4. Providing organisational autonomy
Separating the ET venture from the main corporation so it isn't held back by inertia.
Each solution maps roughly to one trap: peripheral vision counters delayed participation/sticking with the familiar; learning culture counters sticking with the familiar; flexibility counters reluctance to commit; autonomy counters all four by removing big-firm inertia.
Solution 1: Widening peripheral (indirect) vision
First, define which ETs are strategically significant. Strategic projection of an ET's performance potential entails more than a simple linear extrapolation into the future — firms need to estimate the rate of market adoption and the potential size of the market.
PrincipleExplanation
Paint the big pictureAddress the issue of whether the potential market could be "big enough" to support development activities.
Use multiple methodsAll market estimation methods are flawed or limited in some important respect — relying on just one is risky.
Focus on needs, not productsProspective customers can't envision radically new products based on discontinuous innovations, so study the underlying need instead.
Solution 2: Creating a learning culture
Keep learning — but the real challenge is collective (organisational) learning, not just individual learning. There's plenty of "noise" when diverse sources of information are flowing in, so the organisation needs learning capacity built around these characteristics:
1
Encouraging openness to diverse viewpoints — within and across organisational units.
2
Challenge the prevailing mind-set — while facilitating the forgetting of outmoded approaches.
3
Experiment continually — encourage and reward "well-intentioned" failure.
4
Deep dialog — master the skills of strategic conversation.
Solution 3: Staying flexible in strategic ways
The greater the organisation's flexibility, the lower the cost of making a commitment — and the lower the cost of reversing direction. The one downside: too much flexibility may reduce the strategic signaling value of a commitment, which truly requires irreversibility to be credible to rivals and the market. Flexibility needs to be balanced within an options perspective — study rivals intently, and change direction boldly and quickly when needed.
Solution 4: Providing organisational autonomy
Take the ET business outside the main corporation into a separate unit. A separate unit is less held back by inertia, controls, risk-avoidance, and the big-firm mindset that leads to the four traps discussed earlier. Autonomy can have many degrees of separation — up to creating new spin-offs: newly formed companies with their own stock, board, and management team, with the parent retaining some ownership.

Section 3
An Uncertain Future
Why Firms Stand Paralyzed · Why Experts Get It Wrong
Even the experts get it wrong ← memorable exam quote
"I think there is world market for about five computers" — Thomas J. Watson, Chairman of IBM, 1943
Most seasoned experts on a technology have been dead wrong about its future. The real challenge isn't to draw a single, confident future — it's to construct a vision of many possible futures. Often, well-known existing technologies are combined in new ways to recreate innovations, rather than something coming from nowhere.
Why firms stand paralyzed in the face of uncertainty ← direct review Q
Failure of imagination
An inability to make sense of weak signals that don't fit traditional frames of mind.
Failure of leadership
The mind can only see what it is prepared to see — leadership doesn't push the organisation to look further.
Ways to overcome this: recognize opportunities faster than rivals, and use good scenarios to help overcome strategic myopia and frame blindness.

The Power of Scenario Planning
Addressing Uncertainty, Complexity & Paradigm Shift
What scenario planning addresses ← direct review Q, very examinable
ChallengeExplanation
UncertaintyNeed to distinguish this from any form of risk that can be quantified using objective probabilities — uncertainty can't be neatly assigned a probability.
ComplexityForces range from social to technological to economic — scenario planning explores how they combine, intermingle, and dynamically influence one another.
Paradigm shiftScenarios aim to challenge the prevailing mind-set, not just confirm it.
Why "scenario planning" is a good tool ← direct review Q
Allows organisations to examine the interaction between technology and market.
Visualizes the impact of technological discontinuities on the organisation's existing models.
Helps enhance budgeting and resource allocation processes within the firm.
Helps managers better understand the commercial potential of an ET.
Allows organisations to better understand which technology to invest its resources in.
Analyzing weak signals
Scenarios can amplify weak signals. Minor "speciation" events can later produce profound changes in an industry's development. Scenario planning helps track events and make sense of new events as they emerge — catching shifts before they're obvious to everyone.

Section 5
Constructing Scenarios
Step-by-Step Process · Common Traps to Avoid
Steps for constructing scenarios ← direct review Q, very examinable
1
Define the issues you want to understand.
2
Identify the major stakeholders and actors.
3
Identify and study the main forces shaping the future (use STEEP: Social, Technological, Economic, Environmental, Political).
4
Identify trends or predetermined elements that will affect the issues.
5
Identify the key uncertainties from the list of main forces.
6
Select the two most important key uncertainties.
7
Assess the internal consistency and plausibility of the initial learning scenarios.
8
Assess the revised scenarios in terms of how key stakeholders might behave in them.
9
Reexamine the internal consistencies of the learning scenarios.
10
Reassess the uncertainty ranges of the main variables of interest.
STEEP (Social, Technological, Economic, Environmental, Political) is the acronym used at Step 3 to identify the main forces shaping the future — likely to appear as a direct recall question.
Traps to avoid in scenario planning itself ← direct review Q
Scenario planning is unfamiliar territory for many managers, so there are pitfalls in doing it badly:
Failing to get top management support early on
Lack of diverse inputs
Failure to stimulate new strategic options
Not tracking the scenarios' progress & performance

Section 6
Surrogate Crisis, Learning & Decision Scenarios — Conclusion
From Learning Scenarios to Decision Scenarios
Scenario planning as a "surrogate crisis" ← key conceptual point
A real crisis is expensive and often too late to survive. An imagined crisis (scenario) can elicit similar responses to a real crisis, depicting threats and opportunities in vivid detail. This requires an artful balancing of the known and the unknown — enough realism to provoke a genuine reaction, without pretending to predict the unpredictable.
From learning scenarios to decision scenarios
Resistance to new ideas can be reduced if scenarios are treated as learning opportunities rather than predictions. Once learning scenarios are revised and accepted, they can serve as decision scenarios:
Generate new insights about the future environment, so organisations can craft more effective strategies and plans.
Provide a powerful framework for analysing complex and uncertain challenges.
Prepare the organisation not for one single future, but for many uncertainties.
Chapter conclusion ← direct review Q
For established firms to compete, survive, and succeed in industries created or transformed by ETs, they need: senior management support, separation of the new venture from continuing activities, organisational and strategic flexibility, willingness to take risks and learn from experiments, and diverse viewpoints to challenge dominant mind-sets, misleading precedents, and myopic (narrow-minded) views of new ventures.
The 4 traps and 4 solutions covered are not exhaustive — but being aware of them, and tailoring the solutions, helps a firm learn from others' mistakes instead of repeating them. Initiating a scenario planning exercise helps prepare an organisation for real-world situations by building understanding of the benefits of scenario planning and the methods to initiate it.
